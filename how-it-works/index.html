The core idea

Every try-on does the same two jobs

Thirty-plus times a second, without you noticing. The whole difference between our two versions is just who does each job.

JOB 01

Find the face

Watch the live video and work out where the eyes and nose are, and which way the head is turned — right now, and again a moment later as you move.

JOB 02

Draw the glasses

Paint a 3D pair of glasses on top of the video, sized and angled to match that face, so they look like they're really being worn.

The two builds

Assemble the parts, or buy the machine

Version 1 wires several separate tools together by hand. Version 2 hands the whole job to one specialist. Notice how many boxes each one needs.

v1 · MindAR A general face tracker — tracks any face for any purpose. Flexible, but never a perfect fit.
v2 · Jeeliz An engine trained only on faces wearing glasses. Less flexible, dramatically more accurate.
v1 — the assembled chain
01 Camera feed · the browser input The browser asks "may I use the camera?" and hands back live video. It only allows this on secure https:// sites — which caused our phone-testing headaches later.
02 MindAR · the face-finder job 01 Drops 468 invisible dots on your face — eye corners, nose tip, jaw. We pinned the glasses to the dot on the bridge of the nose.
03 A-Frame + three.js · the 3D painter job 02 three.js draws the glasses with light and shadow; A-Frame is the easy shorthand for talking to it, so we write simple tags instead of pages of code.
04 The GLB file · the glasses asset The glasses themselves as a 3D file — think "a JPG, but for a 3D object," carrying its shape, colours and materials.
05 Manual tuning · by hand us No automatic fit. Size and height were nudged by trial and error until the glasses sat on the nose.
result Works, but jittery — a general tracker guesses your head size and depth, so the fit is never quite right.
v2 — the sealed machine
01 Camera feed · the browser input Exactly the same starting point — live video, secure site.
02 · one engine, both jobs
Jeeliz VTO

An AI trained only on faces wearing glasses. Inside one box it:

  • finds the face and measures its real size, so glasses auto-fit — no tuning
  • hides the glasses' arms behind your head so they look real
  • adds true-to-life lighting and reflections on the lenses
03 Model library · real Ray-Bans asset Actual Ray-Ban frames streamed live by product code (a "SKU"). We didn't build them — we just name the ones to show.
04 Our part · the trim us Choose the models, build the scrolling thumbnail bar, style the buttons, fix one bug that froze the "Adjust" screen.
result Accurate, stable, better-looking. Trade-off: it needs internet, and your own custom frames need their paid service.

In one line: for v1 we were the engineers wiring a face-tracker to a 3D painter by hand. For v2 we let a purpose-built glasses-machine do it, and just decorated the outside.

Testing trick

The synthetic face

I can't hold your phone up to check the fit. So I ran an invisible browser on the Mac and secretly fed it a photo of a face pretending to be a webcam, then took screenshots — checking every model and every adjustment without a real person in front of a camera.

Getting it online

Free, secure hosting

Your laptop's local copy can't be opened by your phone. GitHub Pages puts the demo on a real public web address with the secure https:// that cameras require — so any phone just scans a code and it works.
